AAU CET-UG · Exam prep approach

Learning through
beautiful contradictions

You grasp ideas fast — but forget them fast. This system is built around that exact contradiction. Understanding and remembering are different processes. This addresses both.

The core tension
What you do naturally What actually works
Read new chapters, watch videos vs Retrieve old material, fail & correct
Re-read notes (recognition) vs Flashcards without hints (recall)
Block one subject per session vs Mix subjects on purpose (interleaving)
Review before you forget vs Review at the edge of forgetting
The 4-phase approach
01
First contact
Sprint to understand — not to remember
▾
"Read once, fast. Your job is a rough mental model, not perfect recall."

What to do: Read the NCERT section at full speed. Don't annotate. Don't re-read. At the end, close the book and write 3–5 bullet points from memory on a blank page — this is your first retrieval attempt, and it will feel uncomfortable. That discomfort is the learning.

Why it works for you: You grasp fast, so the reading phase doesn't need to be long. The memory dump at the end is where the real encoding starts — because failure to recall is what signals your brain to consolidate.

AAU CET tip: Biology chapters like Genetics and Plant Physiology have heavy terminology. During your dump, write the terms even if you can't define them — the act of writing activates retrieval pathways.

15–20 min/chapter No highlighting Blank-page memory dump
02
Same day
Make one terrible question per concept
▾
"Turn your notes into questions you'll hate yourself for forgetting."

What to do: For each concept, write a question that forces retrieval — not "What is photosynthesis?" but "What gas is released when light hits PS-II?" The answer goes on the back. Make it uncomfortable. Easy questions waste time.

The contradiction: You'll feel like you already know the answer (because you just read it). This feeling is false confidence. Make the card anyway. Tomorrow-you will not remember today-you.

For AAU CET: Focus on mechanism-type questions — "What happens when...", "Why does...", "What is the product of..." — because AAU CET favours application over definition.

20 min Paper flashcards or Anki Mechanism questions only
03
Spaced across days
Review at the edge of forgetting — not before
▾
"Wait until it almost fades. Then retrieve. That gap is the medicine."

What to do: Review flashcards at roughly these intervals: Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14. Each time, attempt the answer before flipping. Cards you got right push to the next interval. Cards you got wrong reset to Day 1.

The beautiful contradiction: Reviewing before you forget does almost nothing. Reviewing at the moment you're about to forget is what drives long-term retention. So intentionally waiting — even when it feels risky — is the correct move.

Setup: Paper: three stacks — "tomorrow", "3 days", "1 week". Shuffle wrong cards back to stack one. Digital: Anki handles the algorithm automatically.

Day 1 → 3 → 7 → 14 Reset wrong cards Anki or 3-stack paper
04
Weekly
Interleave — mix subjects on purpose
▾
"Don't block one subject. Confusion is not a sign you're doing it wrong."

What to do: Once a week, do a 20-question self-test pulling randomly from Biology, Chemistry, and Physics. Don't group by chapter. The switching friction is intentional — it forces your brain to identify the problem type before applying the method.

Why this works: Blocked study (all Biology → all Chemistry) creates an illusion of mastery. Interleaved study is harder and feels less productive, but produces 40–50% better exam performance in timed tests — which is exactly what CET is.

For AAU CET: Physics is your weakest — it benefits most from interleaved practice since concept-switching is where it typically breaks down.

The uncomfortable rule

If reviewing feels easy, it's probably not working. The friction you feel when a flashcard stumps you — or when you can't remember what you read yesterday — is not a failure signal. It's the exact moment consolidation happens. Lean into it.
